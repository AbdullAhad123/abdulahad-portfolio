Contract Breakdown

Thoroughly review every detail outlined in the agreement to ensure a comprehensive understanding of its contents.

  • The scope of work section details the contractor’s services, delineates the project boundaries, and safeguards both the designer and the business. For the designer, it defines the limits of their responsibilities, ensuring they don’t perform tasks or provide deliverables outside the agreed-upon scope. For the business or enterprise, it assures that you won’t charge the designer for any services they didn’t explicitly agree to.

    Meticulously outline every project aspect in this section to prevent scope creep and ensure everyone agrees on the tasks’ execution and costs. For example, if load testing of an ecommerce website is part of the service, the contract must clearly state it, including how this task impacts the overall cost.

  • Specifying clear payment terms establishes a mutual understanding of the financial obligations and expectations. Key elements to include in this section are:

    • Payment schedule Outlining a structured payment schedule provides financial security for the contractor and accountability for the business or enterprise. An example payment structure might look like:- 25% deposit required before starting the project, - Another 25% due at the halfway point, - The final 50% upon project completion‍
    • Additional expenses Detail any potential additional costs, like travel or software subscriptions, that the business is responsible for. Transparency in these costs avoids surprise expenses and provides the contractor with fair compensation for all work.
    • Accepted payment methods Clearly state the accepted payment methods, such as bank transfers, PayPal, or Stripe. This is especially important when dealing with freelance contractors who may prefer specific payment channels.
    • Late payment policies Include any policies related to late payments, like interest on overdue amounts. Maintaining a professional relationship and ensuring timely payments is particularly important for independent contractors who rely on these payments for their livelihood.

  • Incorporate a clear stakeholder feedback and revision process in your web design contract to align expectations between all parties. Here’s how to effectively outline this process

    • Feedback milestones Specify the stages or milestones when the designer will present the work for feedback. For example, the designer might share a website prototype after the initial design phase and provide a specific timeline for the business or enterprise’s team to review and respond. This approach helps keep the project on track and keeps both parties responsible for the project’s direction.
    • Feedback timeline Define a clear timeline for the in-house team to provide feedback to the designer. For example, you might designate five business days for the team to review and respond to a designer’s prototype. Setting a defined period keeps the project progressing without unnecessary delays.
    • Response to delayed feedback Address what happens if the in-house team delays feedback. You might include a clause stating that if the designer doesn’t receive feedback within the agreed-upon time frame, you’ll adjust the project’s delivery date accordingly. This agreement helps manage expectations and adhere to the project’s schedule.
    • Revision rounds Clarify the number of revision rounds included in the project costs to manage the scope of work and prevent the project from becoming mired in endless revisions. State clearly, for example, that the contractor will include two rounds of revisions and any additional revisions will incur extra charges.

  • Every web contract should address potential scenarios where a project may not reach completion and their legal implications.

    Clearly outline the procedures for various termination scenarios, such as:

    • If the service provider cannot complete the work due to unforeseen circumstances and needs to terminate the contract, the contract may state that the designer will deliver all completed work to date and provide a prorated refund for any incomplete portions.
    • If the business decides to halt the project while it’s in progress, the contract might require them to pay the freelancer for the work done up to termination, possibly including a termination fee.
    • In cases where the business fails to meet payment milestones, the contract should specify the consequences, including pausing or terminating the project.

    Additionally, the contract should address legal aspects like indemnification to protect both parties from potential loss or harm resulting from contract termination.

    Specifying the state jurisdiction for legal proceedings is another critical component, especially for businesses in the United States. By outlining jurisdiction in the contract, you stipulate that legal disputes occur in a predetermined location, potentially avoiding escalation to a federal court.

  • Privacy

    A well-drafted web design contract often includes a confidentiality clause or a nondisclosure agreement (NDA) that protects the business’s proprietary information, like trade secrets or unreleased promotions, and instills confidence that their confidential data is secure.

    For example, if a company supplies images for website development, the contract should clearly state that it’s the company’s responsibility to secure all necessary usage licenses and copyright permissions. This provision ensures that the web designer or developer is indemnified against any legal action resulting from copyright infringement should the company provide materials without proper authorization. The same principle applies to other forms of content, such as videos or written copy.

    Force Majeure

    The force majeure clause outlines what happens when unforeseen circumstances or events beyond either party’s control, like natural disasters, wars, or global pandemics, occur. Unlike indemnification, which deals with compensation for losses, force majeure addresses situations where fulfilling contractual obligations becomes impossible due to these external factors.

    Including a force majeure clause protects the designer and the business by specifying that neither party will be liable for failure to meet obligations due to such extraordinary events. This clause also guarantees that in the case of unforeseeable disruptions, both parties clearly understand the terms, and this helps to mitigate potential conflicts or legal disputes arising from unavoidable delays or cancellations.
